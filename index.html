About Me

I am a NASA Einstein postdoctoral fellow in astrophysics working at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory and the California Institute of Technology as part of the TAPIR group.

My research is primarily focused on the development of Bayesian data analysis techniques for pulsar timing array science including gravitational wave detection, noise characterization, non-linear timing, and interstellar medium characterization. I am a member of the North American Nanohertz Observatory for Gravitational Waves (NANOGrav) and the International Pulsar Timing Array (IPTA). Check out my Research page for more information about my work.

I am also very interested in machine learning and data science. I have done some work to classify pulsar and quasar candidates using various machine learning techniques. In my spare time I like to find interesting public datasets and extract valuable information.

Research

Here is a small taste of my research interests. Please see my publications for more details.

Gravitational Waves

Gravitational waves (GWs) are tiny ripples in the fabric of spacetime that are produced by accelerating masses, typically binary systems of compact objects such as black holes, neutron stars, or white dwarfs. GWs are an important prediction of Einstein's General Relativity (GR) and were recently detected by the LIGO Collaboration. Detecting and characterizing GWs across a broad frequency range will allow us to more fully test GR and will open a new observational window into the universe.

Pulsar Timing Arrays

Pulsars are rapidly rotating, highly magnetized neutron stars that emit a beam of radiation along the magnetic field axis. Some pulsars have spin periods of milliseconds and are known as millisecond pulsars (MSPs). The rotations of these pulsars are extremely stable and allow us to use them as clocks. By measuring the train of pulses from the pulsars and constructing a physical model for the pulses, we can learn about the pulsars's orbital dynamics and intrinsic properties, the interstellar medium (ISM), and even fundamental physics and alternative theories of gravity. By observing a collection of MSPs we will be able to measure the spatially correlated effects of GWs and we will gain insights in to the orbital dynamics and environmental effects of supermassive black hole binaries.

Data Analysis

Data analysis is the method by which we make inferences about nature based on a set of observations. In many cases we are presented with noisy or incomplete data and must develop techniques to deal with these obstacles. In many cases, Bayesian analysis is well suited for this task. In other cases we have large data sets that we use to make predictive models for future observations. Several types of machine learning are well suited to this problem.
